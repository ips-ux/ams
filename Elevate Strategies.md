# Strategy Categories

*Each strategy category below is used to add interest, consistency, or variation to your individual sounds or groups.*

- **COMPOSITION**
    
    *The overall arrangement and structuring of a specific type of sound.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - **Bass Variations:** Record variations of your primary bass patch to use for variation or layering in your pattern.
        - **Complementary Leads**: Compose countermelodies that complement the main lead, adding complexity and interest.
- **DISTORTION**
    
    *Processing to add harmonics, distortion, grit, or consistency to a sound.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - **Parallel Distortion**: Blend a distorted signal with the clean snare/clap for subtle grit.
        - **Tube Saturation**: Apply tube saturation for warm, analog distortion.
- **DYNAMICS**
    
    *The control of volume levels in music to enhance its expressiveness and impact.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - **FX Group Limiter:** Use a limiter with default settings onto the FX bus to catch volume peaks, creating a more controlled mix.
        - **Limiter**: Apply a limiter to prevent peaks and ensure consistent volume levels.
- **EQ**
    
    *The adjustment of the balance between different frequency components in an audio signal.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - **Fill EQ:** Try adding subtle low cut during the last 2 or 4 bars of each 8 bars of your drop to help transition to next section.
        - **Cut Boxiness**: Reduce frequencies around 250-500Hz to eliminate boxiness of your vocal.
- **GROOVE**
    
    *The rhythmic feel or sense of swing that makes music compelling and danceable.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - **Finding Groove:** Identify a top loop with groove which fits your track, and extract that groove into your own loop.
        - **Humanization:** Experiment with randomizing velocity of key notes to add humanistic feel.
- **LAYERING**
    
    *The technique of combining multiple sounds or tracks to create a fuller and more complex audio texture.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - **Frequency Layering**: Use layers that occupy different frequency ranges to create a fuller sound.
        - **Top Loops:** Experiment with top loops to identify interesting patterns and placements.
- **PITCH**
    
    *The aspect of sound that determines its highness or lowness, based on the frequency of vibrations.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - **Thickening Sound**: Slightly detune multiple layers of the lead to create a rich, chorusing effect.
        - **Add Body**: Layer your primary vocal pitched an octave down, with a low pass filter to add more body to your vocal.
- **SPATIAL**
    
    *The use of stereo imaging, reverb, and other effects to create a sense of space and depth in the audio.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - **Room Reverb:** Adding ~5-10% of small room reverb can give more real feel, add character.
        - **Removing Lows**: Ensure you’re removing the low frequencies from any reverbs or delays used on basses to minimize potential muddiness.

# Instrument Strategy

## Drums

### **Kick**

- **COMPOSITION**
    - **Reference kick:** Carefully referencing those kicks of a track in the style of your own to ensure compatibility.
    - **Multiple Kicks:** Don’t be afraid to experiment with different kicks in different sections of the song as the track progresses.
    - **Kick Fills:** Double kicks at end of 8-bar section can be unique fill element.
    - **Pre Fills:** Experiment with using double kick as pre-section fill.
    - **Reverse Kick:** Try using reverse kick to lead into your first kick of 8-bar section.
    - **Kick Build**: Use kicks to add energy and tension to your builds.
    - **Kick Removal**: Experiment with removing the last few kicks from an 8-bar or 16-bar section to create impact in the following section.
    - **Impacts**: Use your kicks to accent the beginning of a new section in your break or build.
    - **Kick Length**: Visually assess the length of your kick, ensuring it doesn’t interfere with your bass or sidechain.
    - **Sidechain Alignment:** Visually aligning your kick with your sidechain curve can be useful.
    - **Kick Start**: Visually zoom in on the beginning of your kick, to ensure there is no silence.
    - **Kick Transient:** Adding a sharp decreasing pitch envelope can add a unique transient to the kick.
    - **Smooth Release**: Use a smooth release for a natural fade-out.
    - **ADSR Automation**: Automate ADSR settings for evolving kicks.
- **DISTORTION**
    - **Erosion:** Use Erosion plugin to add subtle high end to your kick when necessary.
    - **Saturation:** Subtle saturation or distortion on your kick can help give it a dirtier feel.
    - **Melodic Saturation:** Try saturating the root note of your kick to create tonal distortion.
    - **Tube Distortion**: Apply tube distortion for analog warmth.
    - **Tape Saturation**: Use tape saturation for a vintage feel.
    - **Bitcrushing**: Apply bitcrushing for a lo-fi effect.
    - **Transient Distortion**: Apply distortion to the transient for a punchy attack.
    - **Distortion Layers**: Layer distorted kicks with clean kicks.
    - **Clipper Plugins**: Use clipper plugins to limit peaks and add distortion.
- **DYNAMICS**
    - **Transient Shaping:** Boost the transient by 10-20% with a transient shaper to add punch, knock.
    - **Anchor Kick:** Keep your kick drum volume static when mixing to help create consistency and avoid circular mixing.
    - **Compressing Kick:** Try compressing your kick to increase body and help it sit better in the mix.
    - **Parallel Compression**: Blend compressed and uncompressed kicks.
    - **Multiband Compression**: Apply compression to specific frequency ranges.
    - **Sidechain Compression**: Use sidechain compression to duck other elements.
    - **Gate**: Use a gate to tighten the kick.
    - **Soft Knee Compression**: Use soft knee settings for smooth compression.
- **EQ**
    - **Tune Kick:** Use EQ or Tuner to verify key of kick matches song.
    - **EQ Boost:** Use an EQ boost on a kick’s fundamental frequency to make it sound more tonal.
    - **EQ Dip:** Remove frequencies where snare/clap hits can be helpful for opening space.
    - **EQ Cut:** Remove frequencies where bass is hitting to create space in the low end.
    - **Low-Cut:** Remove low-end from your kick during intro/buildup to make drop kick more impactful.
    - **High-Cut:** Experiment with remove high frequencies from your kick during the intro or break.
    - **EQ Trick**: Try low cutting your EQ during the last 2 to 4 bars of a section, helping to transition energy to the next section.
    - **Frequency Sweeps**: Perform frequency sweeps to identify and correct issues.
    - **Pultec Trick**: Use the Pultec EQ trick to boost and attenuate the same frequency for a unique effect.
    - **Dynamic EQ**: Apply EQ dynamically to adapt to changes in the kick.
- **LAYERING**
    - T**ransient Layer:** Try layering a transient or “tok” at beginning of your kick for punch.
    - **Transient Emphasis**: Use transient designers to control attack and sustain phases.
    - **Stomp layer:** Layering an additional “stomp” kick on the first kick of a drop section can help add impact.
    - **White Noise**: Quiet white noise shaped around the envelope of your kick can help add high end information.
    - **Two Kicks:** Try layering a full-bodied kick under a crispier, high end kick. Blend them with EQ.
    - **Acoustic Layer**: Use an acoustic kick layer for natural textures.
    - **EQ Layering**: EQ each layer individually for a balanced sound.
    - **Pure Sine Wave**: Add a sub layer using a pure sine wave to enhance low-end presence.
    - **Percussion Sample**: Layer a percussive sample like a snare or tom to add texture.
    - **Reverb Layer**: Add a reverb-processed kick layer for space and depth.
    - **Reverse Reverb**: Use a reversed reverb tail from a kick sample to create a swell effect.
- **PITCH**
    - **Tune Kick**: Ensure your kick is cohesive with the tonality of your track and underlying bassline.
    - **Kick Build**: Experiment with pitching your kicks up an octave over the course of your build.

### **Clap/Snare**

- **COMPOSITION**
    - **Reverse claps:** Try using reverse before every other clap (foley can be useful).
    - **Fills**: Use your existing clap or snare to build interesting fills, and layer them with different instruments.
    - **Rolls**: Integrate snare rolls or fills to transition between sections.
    - **Accent Shifts**: Vary the accents on different hits to emphasize certain beats.
    - **Flams**: Use flams, where two quick hits are played in rapid succession, to add a human touch.
    - **Silence Gaps**: Introduce brief moments of silence to create tension and release.
- **DISTORTION**
    - **Parallel Distortion**: Blend a distorted signal with the clean snare/clap for subtle grit.
    - **Tube Saturation**: Apply tube saturation for warm, analog distortion.
    - **Tape Saturation**: Use tape saturation to add a vintage feel.
    - **Bitcrushing**: Apply bitcrushing for a lo-fi, gritty texture.
    - **Soft Clipping**: Employ soft clipping to gently distort peaks.
    - **Hard Clipping**: Use hard clipping for aggressive, punchy distortion.
    - **Overdrive**: Apply overdrive for a warm, driven sound.
    - **Distortion with Reverb**: Apply distortion after reverb for a unique effect.
    - **Transient Distortion**: Distort only the transient part of the sound for a sharp attack.
- **DYNAMICS**
    - **Parallel Compression**: Blend a heavily compressed duplicate with the original clap/snare for added punch and sustain.
    - **Fast Attack and Release Settings**: Use fast attack and release to tighten the transient and enhance presence.
    - **Sidechain Compression**: Duck the clap/snare with sidechain compression triggered by another element like the kick drum to create space.
    - **Multiband Compression**: Apply compression to specific frequency bands to enhance clarity without affecting the entire signal.
    - **Brickwall Limiting**: Cap the peak level to keep the clap/snare within a controlled dynamic range.
    - **Soft Limiting**: Use a soft limiter for subtle control over peaks while maintaining natural sound.
    - **Peak Limiting**: Focus on controlling the highest peaks to prevent clipping and distortion.
- **EQ**
    - **Low-cut:** Ensure low end of clap/snare is carved out for kick if being layered.
    - **Tonal Boost**: Try boosting the fundamental frequency of your clap or snare to make it more tonal.
    - **Stereo Boost:** Try boosting the sides of your clap or stereo using a mid/side EQ to add width.
    - **Midrange Boost**: Boost midrange frequencies to add presence.
    - **Presence Boost**: Enhance the 3-5kHz range for clarity.
    - **Cut Boxiness**: Cut around 250-500Hz to reduce boxiness.
    - **Wide Q Adjustments**: Use wide Q settings for broad, smooth changes.
    - **Narrow Q Adjustments**: Use narrow Q settings for precise, targeted cuts.
- **GROOVE**
    - **Automated Groove:** Add Groove and velocity automations to make your drum builds more interesting.
    - **Pre-Shifted Claps: U**se pre-shifted claps ahead of main hit for interesting character.
    - **Align Delay:** Experiment with moving clap back or forward in time for different feel.
    - **Velocity Humanization:** Use random variations in velocity and groove to make your snare builds more realistic.
    - **Length Variation:** Automating length of clap on different hits can create a fun rhythm.
    - **Flam Timing**: Vary the timing of flams to add rhythmic interest.
    - **Accent Patterns**: Use varying accent patterns to emphasize different beats.
    - **Interactive Rhythms**: Create interactive rhythms that play off other elements.
    - **Off-Beat Claps**: Place claps slightly off-beat to create a laid-back feel.
- **LAYERING**
    - **Multiple Claps**: Layer multiple clap samples for a fuller sound.
    - **Snare and Clap Combo**: Combine snare and clap hits for a unique texture.
    - **Stereo Spread**: Spread different layers across the stereo field.
    - **Vocal Layer:** Layering short vocal shots can add organic character.
    - **Transient Layering**: Add a layer to emphasize the transient.
    - **Add Foley: L**ayer in subtle foley or sound effects to add texture
    - **Fill Idea:** Use your main clap/snare in your fills as a layer to create congruence.
    - **Crowd Claps:** Use group claps in a build to add energy.
    - **White Noise:** Try adding a subtle layer of white noise to fill out the high end of your clap or snare.
    - **Reverb Layer**: Experiment with layering in a long hall reverb layer clap/snare on the first hit of an 8-bar or 4-bar section.
    - **Midrange Layer**: Use a midrange-focused layer for fullness.
    - **Processed Layer**: Layer a heavily processed sound for unique texture.
- **PITCH**
    - **Tuning:** Play with snare/clap pitch to find what works best with your kick.
    - **Layering**: Try adding a layer pitched down an octave every 4 hits to create interest.
    - **Snare Build:** Experiment with pitching your snare up over the course of the build to increase energy.
    - **Fills:** Try different pitching up/down patterns during your fill for additional complexity.
    - **Pitch Shifting**: Shift the pitch of claps/snares for variation.
    - **Detuned Layers**: Slightly detune layers for a chorusing effect.
    - **Reverse Pitch Shifts**: Reverse the pitch shift for unique effects.
- **SPATIAL**
    - **Short Reverb**: Use short reverb for a tight, punchy sound.
    - **Long Reverb**: Apply long reverb for an expansive effect.
    - **Pre-Delay**: Use pre-delay to separate the hit from the reverb tail.
    - **Reverb Variations:** Adding long hall reverb as clap layer for first hit in 4-bar or 8-bar section can be fun.
    - **Stereo Widening:** Spread clap wider if fewer elements are playing at that time
    - **Reverse Reverb Tail:** Resample a reverb tail from your snare to use as a reverse sweep.
    - **Gated Reverb**: Apply gated reverb for a controlled reverb tail.
    - **Room Reverb**: Use room reverb to simulate a small space.
    - **Hall Reverb**: Apply hall reverb for a grand, spacious sound.
    - **Plate Reverb**: Use plate reverb for a classic, bright tone.
    - **Spring Reverb**: Apply spring reverb for a vintage feel.
    - **Convolution Reverb**: Use convolution reverb with unique impulse responses.
    - **Ping-Pong Delay**: Apply ping-pong delay for rhythmic movement.
    - **Tempo-Synced Delay**: Use delay synced to the track’s tempo.

    - **Parallel Distortion**: Blend a distorted signal with the clean drum bus for added texture.
    - **Tape Saturation**: Apply tape saturation for a warm, analog feel.
    - **Tube Saturation**: Use tube saturation to add harmonic richness and warmth.
    - **Bitcrushing**: Apply bitcrushing for a gritty, lo-fi effect.
    - **Overdrive**: Use overdrive to add intensity and drive.
    - **Wave Shaping**: Experiment with wave shaping for unique harmonic distortion.

    - **Limiter**: Apply a limiter to prevent peaks and ensure consistent volume levels.

    - **Transient Punch:** Use Transient Shaper to give your group extra punch.

    - **High Shelf:** Use a high shelf to boost the highs by 1-3db to add sparkle.
    - **High-End Boost**: Enhance high frequencies for clarity and brightness.

## Bass

- **COMPOSITION**
    - **Variations:** Record variations of your primary bass patch to use for variation or layering in your pattern.
    - **Call and Response**: Implement call and response patterns with other elements.
    - **Melodic Layers**: Experiment with a simple sub bass line, with a more complex rhythmic bassline in the same key above it.
    - **Drop Impact:** Avoid using too much low bass at the beginning of your song, in order to increase impact of your drop later on.
    - **Filter Breaks:** If using the same bass in the breaks or verses of your song, add subtle low cut to increase impact of your bass in the drop.
    - **Groove Alignment**: Ensure the swing of your bassline matches that of your drums, synths, and vocals.
    - **Octave Jumps**: Use octave jumps to add movement and excitement.
    - **Rests and Silence**: Include rests and pauses to create space and dynamics.
    - **Rhythmic Fills**: Add fills to transition between sections or bars.
    - **Alternate Patterns**: Switch between different bass patterns or phrasing within a track.
    - **Inversions**: Use chord inversions in the bassline for harmonic variation.

# Automation Strategy

- **VOLUME**
    
    *Use gradual increases or decreases in volume to add tension or release.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - Automate lead volume to increase over the course of 8 or 16 bars.
        - Slowly increasing the volume of your primary melody over the course of your build.
        - Decreasing tonal sounds and background effects throughout your outro.
- **PANNING**
    
    *Move sounds across stereo field for a more interesting listening experience.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - Automate the panning of percussion shots throughout your drop.
        - Use automation to mix vocal chants across the right and left sides of the stereo field.
- **VELOCITY**
    
    *Experiment with changes in velocity on your key sounds.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - Create variation in the velocity of specific percussion hits in your drum pattern.
        - Vary the velocity of specific piano hits in your chord progression to add humanization and impact.
- **FILTERS**
    
    *Automate filter cutoff frequencies to gradually add or remove harmonics over time.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - Slowly open a low pass filter to introduce your lead throughout your build.
        - Opening a low pass filter on your bass throughout your intro to introduce the sound.
- **LFOs**
    
    *Use variation in LFO rate, depth, and shape to create evolving textures.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - Reduce the LFO depth on a stutter vocal toward the last 8-bars of your drop.
        - Increase the rate of your LFO on your lead sustained sound over the course of your build.
- **PLUGIN PARAMETERS**
    
    *Experiment with automating the parameters on your plugin or effect over time.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - Automate wavetable position on your lead throughout the drop to create variation.
        - Increase the spread of voices over time on a pad preset.
- **SPATIAL**
    
    *Automate delay parameters to create evolving echoes and complex rhythmic patterns.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - Increase the dry/wet and decay of a reverb over the course of your build.
        - Slowly add dry/wet to the delay on your vocal chants over time.
- **DISTORTION**
    
    *Increase or decrease distortion to add grit and intensity during key moments.*
    
    - **Examples**
        - Use more distortion on your vocal phrase throughout your drop than in your break.
        - Increase distortion on your leads for the second 8-bars of your drop.
- **STEREO WIDTH**
    
    Automate stereo width to make elements feel more narrow or wide at different points.
    
    - **Examples**
        - Utilize panning of individual sounds across the stereo field.
        - Create width variation and contrast with the primary sounds used in your drop.

# Final Checks

- **Section Flow**
    
    *Do all transitions feel intentional?*
    
    → If a section feels jarring or disconnected, it likely needs a better buildup, riser, or variation to bridge the gap.
    
- **Energy Balance**
    
    *Does the energy rise and fall in a way that makes sense?*
    
    → Check if any part feels too flat or overly chaotic compared to the rest.
    
- **Motif + Theme Consistency**
    
    Are your main ideas (melodies, rhythm, sound palette) carried through the entire track?
    
    → Repetition builds familiarity, but variation keeps it interesting.
    
- **Contrast + Payoff**
    
    Does your drop hit after the buildup?
    
    → If the drop doesn’t feel impactful, the build may need more tension — or the drop itself might need cleaner arrangement or mix decisions.
    
- **Mix Clarity**
    
    Can you clearly hear all core elements (kick, bass, lead, vocals)?
    
    → If not, consider re-EQing, adjusting levels, or simplifying layers.
    
- **Does It Drag?**
    
    Are there any moments where your attention drifts?
    
    → If yes, tighten it up, automate movement, or remove redundant sections.
    
- **Emotional Impact**
    
    Does the song feel like what you intended?
    
    → If not, ask yourself what emotion is missing — and which element (harmony, sound, rhythm) could bring it out.
    
- **Is Anything Missing?**
    
    Often, it’s not about adding more — it’s about refining what's already there.
    

# When You’re Stuck

*Use this section to break out of creative paralysis and get your track across the finish line. Don’t aim for perfect. Aim for done.*

### Reframe the Goal

- Ask yourself: **“What’s the simplest version of this track that still works?”**
- Shift from perfecting to publishing. Finished is better than flawless.
- Imagine you're submitting this to a label or playing it live in 2 hours. What needs to happen now?

### Quick-Finish Tactics

- **Cut the Fat**: Delete any elements that aren’t adding energy, emotion, or purpose.
- **Skip the Mix**: Don’t obsess over EQs or compressors yet. Rough bounce first.
- **Loop Section First**: Focus on one key section (drop, breakdown, etc.) and expand outward.
- **Automate One Thing**: Add one automation lane to create movement (volume, filter, reverb, etc.).
- **Use Templates**: Drag your idea into an arrangement template and let structure guide you.

### Urgency Exercises

- **The Ultra Method**: Imagine you have to play the track at Ultra in 2 hours. What stays? What needs fixing now?
- **30-Minute Challenge**: Set a timer. No distractions. Make bold decisions quickly.
- **Limit Your Tools**: Use only 3 instruments/effects to finish the track. Constraint creates clarity.

### Mindset Reset

- Done is better than perfect.
- A finished track teaches you more than ten half-finished ones.
- You can always revisit and revise later. Right now, just finish *this version.*

### Mini Checklist: Is It Actually Stuck?

- Is the structure complete?
- Are the core ideas present (drums, bass, lead, vocal, etc.)?
- Do transitions get the job done?
- Are you delaying because you're unsure—or just afraid to finish?
