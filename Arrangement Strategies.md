# Strategies by Section

## Intro

*Sets the mood and tone, gradually introducing elements. This is often the “Extended Mix” portion of the track which allows DJ’s to mix seamless into the song.*

- **LENGTH**
    
    16-32 bars
    
- **STRATEGIES**
    - **Drum Progression**: Slowly progress your drum layering during this section.
    - **High Pass Filtering**: Do not start with full low end introduced.
    - **Set Mood**: Use spatial sounds from your primary sections to immediately set the mood for your song.
    - **Width Automation**: Minimize stereo width to add impact to your drop.
    - **Mixability**: Make it easy for DJ’s to mix into your track by removing major melodic or vocal elements in this section.
    - **Set Groove**: Ensure your intro elements set the groove for what is to come.
    - **Use atmospheric sounds**: Add ambient pads or effects to create a mood.
    - **Filter sweeps**: Apply low-pass or high-pass filters that open up gradually.
    - **Subtle melodies**: Introduce a soft, simple melody or motif.
    - **Vocal chops**: Use small vocal snippets to grab attention.
    - **Percussive elements**: Add light percussion to add interest without overwhelming.
    - **Reversed sounds**: Reverse samples or synths for an intriguing effect.
    - **Ambient noise**: Use natural or urban soundscapes to set the scene.
    - **Echoes and delays**: Add delay effects to create space.
    - **Automated panning**: Move sounds from left to right to create a dynamic stereo field.
    - **Minimalist approach**: Keep it sparse and build complexity gradually.

## Build

*This section introduces key musical elements while building tension, anticipation, excitement, and mystery for what is to come in the next section.*

- **LENGTH**
    
    8-24 bars
    
- **STRATEGIES**
    - **Sound Automation**: Use filter, envelope, and wavetable automations on key sounds to build tension.
    - **Tease Melody**: Introducing small hints of your melody before it fully drops makes it feel more familiar when it arrives.
    - **Risers**: Use one or multiple layered riser samples.
    - **Filter Automation**: Open or close filters on primary sounds.
    - **Pitch Automation**: Increase pitch of build sounds by an octave.
    - **Harmonization**: Add additional harmonic layers or octaves to your progression.
    - **Vocal Shouts**: Place vocal shout samples with spatial effects.
    - **White Noise**: Add white noise sample to fill out your build.
    - **Stereo Automation**: Slowly narrow the stereo width to make drop feel more full.
    - **Drum Build**: Create a drum build with snares or kicks to build tension.
    - **Progression Variation**: Add variance or layering to your primary chord progression.
    - **Build Layering**: Introduce additional layers to your primary break or build sounds.
    - **Reverse Reverb**: Resample reverb on existing sounds from your drop to tease their tonality in the build.
    - **Vocal Build**: Create a repeating section of existing vocals, increasing the rate over the course of the build.
    - **Drum Roll**: Add a drum build with increasing frequency, pitch, or dry/wet on interesting spatial effects.
    - **White Noise**: Use white noise risers to fill out the frequency spectrum of your build.
    - **Glitches**: Experiment with glitch samples or loops to use throughout your build.
    - **Vocal Shouts**: Collect samples of variation spatial vocal shouts which match the vibe of your track, and place them in your build to add energy and impact.
    - **Stabs**: Insert synth stabs or one shots with spatial effects to add impact.
    - **Drop Filtering**: Slowly introduce primary drop sounds using opening low pass filter automation.
    - **Stutter Reduction**: Reduce the dry/wet on a stutter effect used in your intro.
    - **Low Cut Automation**: Slowly reduce low frequencies over the course of your build.
    - **Cinematic FX**: Add cinematic stabs and orchestral hits to add impact.
    - **DJ Controller**: Experiment with DJ-like effects you would use in a live show.
    - **Delay Build**: Increase dry/wet on a delay while reducing the time to create an interesting build effect.
    - **Build FX**: Experiment with build plugins like Endless Smile, Infinite, OneKnob
    - **Drum Automation**: Increase the rate of drums like kicks or snares to increase build
    - **Drum Chops**: Experiment with shorter drum fills to break up specific sections of your build.
    - **Volume Automation**: Create volume automation reduce Master volume by 1-4dB over course of build
    - **Progressive Layering**: Create progression on your drum layering, such as additional open hats or rides.
    - **Introduce Lows**: Slowly introduce low end toward the end of your drop through lowering your high pass filter.

## Drop

*The high-energy climax where the main bassline, drums, and lead elements come together to create maximum impact and release the tension created in your build.*

- **LENGTH**
    
    16-32 bars
    
- **STRATEGIES**
    - **Primary Melody**: Introduce the main melody or hook of your song.
    - **Vocal Hooks**: Incorporate catchy vocal phrases to add energy.
    - **Full Stereo Image**: Use stereo widening or stereo layering for a fuller sound.
    - **Layered Drums**: Add layers to your drum kit for extra punch and fullness.
    - **Synth Stabs**: Use aggressive synth stabs to create energy and impact.
    - **Sidechain Compression**: Use sidechain compression for a pulsing effect and to create a clean kick-bass relationship.
    - **Saturation**: Add distortion to make elements more aggressive.
    - **Glitch Effects**: Apply glitch effects to certain elements to create contrast.
    - **Synth Fills**: Add short synth fills between phrases to add variation to your call-and-response pattern.
    - **Filtering**: Add a low pass filter on the final 1-2 bars of a section to remove low and create impact for the following section.
    - **Bass Variation**: Add variation to your bass pattern or layering to keep it interesting.
    - **Drum Removal**: Remove low end drum elements on the final 1-2 bars of a section to remove low and create impact for the following section.
    - **Pitch Bending**: Use pitch bends or portamento for a dynamic feel.
    - **FX Hits**: Add impactful sound effects such as exhausts or shouts, to help add impact and release tension.
    - **Spatial Layering**: Use sounds which fill out the full 3-dimension space of your mix, such as reverb shots at specific points in your melody.
    - **Filter Modulation**: Modulate filters for movement.
    - **Synth Modulation**: Add subtle movement in parameters like wavetable position and dry/wet effects to create variation in your synth pattern.

## Break

*A lower-energy section that contrasts with the drop, often featuring softer, melodic elements and creating space while still building tension to keep the listener engaged.*

- **LENGTH**
    
    8-16 bars
    
- **STRATEGIES**
    - **New Melody**: Create a new complementary which is congruent with drop bassline.
    - **Loop Samples**: Experiment with different loop samples in key of your song to create interest.
    - **Repurpose Melody**: Apply your existing melody to new, softer sounds to offer contrast from your drop.
    - **Stutter Effect**: Add a stutter or LFO effect to a sustained sound in key.
    - **Time Variation**: Try slowing down your primary melody, to half speed or quarter speed, with new, softer sounds.
    - **Evolving Sound**: Create a slowly evolving, growing sound to build tension.
    - **Chord Progression**: Create a progression with underlying notes which align with primary bassline.
    - **Transitions**: Use white noise or cymbal transitions to lead into your break.
    - **Impacts**: Utilize impact samples to signal beginning or different sections of your break.
    - **Atmospheres**: Layer sustained atmospheric sounds in the key of your song.
    - **Filtered Drums**: Experiment with low-pass and high-pass filters on a drum groove to maintain a rhythm to keep listeners dancing.
    - **Drum Loops**: Try using different types of drum loops, such as breakbeats, to fill out your break.
    - **Strip Back**: Begin with removing layers from your drop to create the foundation for your break.
    - **Spatial FX**: Add reverbs, delays, and echos, possibly with filter automations, to primary sounds.
    - **Reverse Reverbs**: Use resampled reverbs to add gliding and sucking effects into different sections.
    - **Sirens**: Experiment with alarms and sirens underneath your break to add energy and tension.
    - **Vocal Chops**: Cut words or phrases with your primary vocal, with added spatial effects and filtering.
    - **Crashes**: Add sustained percussion hits, such as crashes, to fill out high end of your break.
    - **Reese Bass**: Add a reese bass to fill out the underlying low end of your break.
    - **Drone Sub**: Add sustained low end sub to help fill out your low end.
    - **New Melody**: Create a new melody with slight variations on your existing sounds.
    - **Low Pass Filter**: Add a slowly opening low pass filter underneath your existing melody structure.
    - **Chord Progression**: Build a chord progression using certain notes in the bassline as root notes.
    - **Ambience**: Collect and insert ambience samples in the root key of your song.
    - **Strip Back**: Remove elements from your drop one by one to simplify the layers within your break.
    - **Harmonization**: Introduce a new harmony over your existing melody to add variation.
    - **Resample**: Heavily process and resample primary sounds used in your drop to create more spatial variations for your breaks.
    - **Chord Variation**: Create a variation or progression within your chord progression.
    - **Pitch Automation**: Add pitch modulation of existing sounds along with filtering to create variation.
    - **Transitions**: Experiment with transition sounds to lead into your break and at different points.
    - **Spatial Processing**: Experiment with adding delays and echos, along with filter automation, on primary sounds used in your drop.
    - **Percussion Loops**: Add rhythmic percussion loops with an opening filter automation on them to maintain groove during your breaks.
    - **Vocal FX**: Add rhythmic spatial effects to words or phrases of primary vocal.
    - **Orchestral**: Introduce sustained string or orchestral elements to add organic textures to your break.
    - **Pads**: Use sustained pads which follow your root note or specific melodic variations in your primary melody.
    - **Granular Synthesis**: Add granular synthesis processing to key samples in your break.
    - **Soundscapes**: Layer organic ambience or recordings to create foundation.
    - **Filtered Tease**: Resample individual filtered shots of primary drop, with filter automation and spatial effects.
    - **Stutter Effect**: Add stutter effect to sustained sounds from your drop or otherwise.
    - **Reese Bass**: Use a Reese bass to fill out your low end and tease certain sections of notes from your primary melody.
    - **Rhythmic Pucks**: Add rhythmic one shots in the root key of your sound.
    - **Reuse Melody**: Drag your primary MIDI to a new MIDI channel, and experiment with softer sounds that could be used to tease this melody in the break.
    - **Drones**: Introduce drone sounds in root key of your track to fill out the high end of your spectrum.
    - **Analog**: Use analog synth pads to tease specific notes of your primary melody.
    - **Arpeggiator**: Introduce arpeggiator in your breaks to create supporting melody.
    - **Kicks**: Use your kick to signify the beginning of a 4-bar or 8-bar section.
    - **Impacts**: Use impacts at beginning and at other key points of your break.
    - **Filtered drums**: Use filtered drum loops to maintain rhythm without intensity.

## Bridge

*A transitional section that introduces new themes or variations and connects different parts of the track while building tension for the following build.*

- **LENGTH**
    
    8-16 bars
    
- **STRATEGIES**
    - **New Themes**: Add new melodic or harmonic elements.
    - **Rhythmic Change**: Alter the rhythm or tempo slightly.
    - **Harmonic Variation**: Introduce new chords or progressions.
    - **Instrumental Focus**: Highlight a specific instrument or sound.
    - **Build Anticipation**: Use risers and effects to build towards the next section.
    - **Texture Changes**: Switch up textures to keep the listener engaged.
    - **Vocal Harmonies**: Add harmonies to vocal lines for added variation and interest.
    - **Subtle Risers**: Use soft risers to add anticipation.
    - **Percussion Breakdown**: Focus on percussive elements.
    - **Dynamic Contrast**: Create a dynamic contrast with volume changes.
    - **Melodic Interplay**: Use counter-melodies for complexity.
    - **Layering**: Add new layers or subtract existing ones.
    - **Filter Sweeps**: Use filter sweeps creatively.
    - **Ambient Sounds**: Incorporate ambient or environmental sounds.
    - **Reverse Elements**: Use reversed sounds for an interesting effect.
    - **Spatial Effects**: Use reverb and delay to create space in this section.
    - **Automated Panning**: Pan elements left and right for a dynamic stereo field.
    - **Harmonic Tension**: Use dissonant chords to create tension.
    - **Build Energy**: Gradually increase energy towards the next section.

## 2nd Build

 *A section that reintroduces and intensifies elements from the first build, building additional tension, excitement, and anticipation for the 2nd drop.*

- **LENGTH**
    
    16-32 bars
    
- **STRATEGIES**
    - **Intensify elements**: Increase the intensity of elements used in the first build.
    - **New Layers**: Introduce new layers or sounds.
    - **Rhythm Variation**: Change up the rhythm to keep it fresh.
    - **Vocal Chops**: Use vocal chops creatively.
    - **Pitch Risers**: Gradually increase the pitch of key elements.
    - **Filter Automation**: Use filter sweeps and automation to gradually introduce or fade elements in the mix.
    - **Snare Rolls**: Incorporate snare rolls that build in speed.
    - **Noise Sweeps**: Add white noise sweeps to fill out the frequency content of your build.
    - **Dynamic EQ**: Boost certain frequencies.
    - **Stutter Effects**: Apply stutter effects to sustained sounds you may have used in your break or bridge to create additional tension.
    - **Tension Chords**: Use dissonant chords to create greater tension.
    - **Volume Swells**: Increase volume gradually to build intensity, while ensuring your volume doesn’t overpower your drop.
    - **Countdown Effects**: Use countdown samples or similar vocal phrases to build tension.
    - **Sub-Drop Silence**: Introduce a sub-drop, followed by a moment of silence before the drop.
    - **Spatial Effects**: Increase reverb and delay to wash out elements of your drop towards the end, in order to create impact during the drop that follows.
    - **Percussive Build**: Layer percussive elements into your build to create tension.
    - **Layered Risers**: Use multiple risers or rising effects to create anticipation.
    - **Melodic Anticipation**: Introduce the melody softly through volume or filter automation, foreshadowing what is to come for the listener.

    - **Risers**: Use short, intense risers to create another moment of tension before your drop.
    - **Percussive Hit**: Add a strong percussive hit, possibly with spatial effects to create an element of space.
    - **Filter Sweep**: Use a quick filter sweep.
    - **Volume Dip**: Lower the volume momentarily.
    - **Delay and Reverb**: Use these effects creatively.
    - **Glitch Effects**: Apply glitch effects to create unique textures or rhythms with your primary elements.
    - **Sub-Drop**: Use a sub-drop for impact.
    - **Drum Fill**: Add a quick drum or snare fill to build tension for the drop.
    - **Melodic Snippet**: Use a short melodic phrase.

    - **Pitch Riser**: Use a quick pitch riser.
    - **Noise Sweep**: Add a noise sweep.
    - **Harmonic Change**: Change the harmony briefly.

# Tension + Release Strategies

### **TENSION**

*Use the strategies below to create feelings of **excitement** and **anticipation** for what is to come.*

- **Pitch**
    - **Strategies**
        - **Pitch Bend**: Create a increase or decrease in the pitch of a sound over the course of your build.
        - **Octave Change**: Go up or down an octave in your main melody, or layering in additional octaves of your existing melody.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Focus on increasing in octaves to ensure it starts and ends in the key of your song.
        - Ideally have sounds with pitch bends increasing at the same rate, so they stay in key.
- **Filter**
    - **Strategies**
        - **Open Filter**: Slowly open a lowpass filter on primary instruments (synths, drums, ect) over the course of your build.
        - **High Pass Filter**: Remove low frequencies from your build elements to help create tension. Ensure you don’t remove so much low end that your section loses power.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Ensure you don’t reduce frequencies so much that your song loses energy.
        - When using automation to open your filter, ensure your high frequency content doesn’t overwhelm that in the following section.
        - Maintain adequate amounts of low end frequencies in your build to maintain energy and power.
- **Volume**
    - **Strategies**
        - **Volume Reduction**: Slowly reduce overall volume by 1-4dB over the course of your build.
        - **Instrument Fade**: Automate the volume of drum, bass, or melodic elements down over the course of your build, as to add impact to your drop.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Do not reduce your volume too much during your build, as your build will lose energy drop will sound too abrasive.
        - Ensure rhythmic elements have adequate volume to keep listeners dancing and moving in the club.
- **Stereo Width**
    - **Strategies**
        - **Narrow Stereo Field**: Use automation to steadily increase or decrease the stereo width of a sound over the course of your build.
        - **Stereo Evolution**: Allow specific sounds to widen during your build, then narrow again toward the end of your build.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Don’t make your build too narrow, as it will lose energy for the listener.
        - Narrowing individual elements can be more effective and interesting than narrowing your track as a whole.
- **Envelope**
    - **Strategy**
        - **Envelope Opening**: Use automation to steadily open up the envelope of the sound over the course of your build.
        - **Envelope Shortening**: Slowly shorten the envelope of your lead or synth during your build.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Significant contrast in your envelope shape between build and drop is very effective.
        - Be wary of adding too much decay to sounds playing a diverse melody, as it can become messy.
- **Sound Design**
    - **Strategy**
        - **Wavetable Automation**: Vary the wavetable position of your synth or preset throughout the course of your build.
        - **FX Automation**: Increase the dry/wet or other parameters on effects used on your preset.
        - **Layer Introduction**: Slowly introduce an additional layer to your primary melody over the course of your build.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Ensure any modifications or layers you add don’t compromise the overall quality of your primary sound.
- **FX**
    - **Strategy**
        - **Wash Out**: Use a combination of increasing reverb and delay to slowly wash out sounds in your build, before they come in fully drop during your drop.
        - **Delay**: Steadily increase the dry/wet on a delay throughout your build, while also experimenting with shortening or lengthening the time parameter as well.
        - **Distortion:** Slowly increase the amount of distortion used on a sound, or group of sounds, throughout your build.
        - **Phaser/Flanger**: Increasing the amount of phaser or flanger on a sound can be useful in creating variation and tension in that sound.
        - **FX Plugins**: Experiment with FX plugins like Endless Smile to steadily create spatial variation on a sound or group of sounds throughout your build.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Ensure that when using spatial effects, you don’t overly wash out your sounds as they may lose interest.
        - Ensure you maintain adequate low end under any sounds which may be getting washed out so that your drop retains adequate power.
- **Samples**
    - **Strategy**
        - **Risers**: Layer a riser or uplifter throughout the entire course of your build.
        - **Reverse Reverb**: Use resampled reverb tails from primary sounds to build tension.
        - **Drum Roll**: Use a riser drum build or drum roll to create tension throughout your build.
        - **Drum Fills**: Use drum fills to build energy and identify different sections of your build.
        - **White Noise**: Use white noise to fill out the frequency content of your build.
        - **Alarms**: Layer in alarms or sirens to create a hype and energetic vibe.
        - **Vocal Chants**: Begin inserting vocal chants from your drop into your build to add hype.
        - **Rising Ambience**: Utilize a sustained ambience sample, pitching up an octave over the course of your build, to build tension.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Do not rely solely on samples to build tension, as this will feel artificial and amateur.
        - Ensure any tonal FX are within the key of your track.
        - Be careful to ensure the combination of all your FX doesn’t overwhelm the power of the following section.
        - Ensure rhythmic FX samples align with the rhythm of your track as a whole.
- **Music Theory**
    - Melody playing notes away from root note, then resolving and creating release when returning to root note.
- **Composition**
    - **Strategy**
        - **Octave Layering**: Slowly introduce an additional octave of a sound or melody throughout your build.
        - **Low Layers**: Bring in a lower layer of your sound to bring fullness to a later part of your build.
        - **Rhythmic Layering**: Steadily introduce a rhythmic sound which supports your primary cadence throughout your build.
        - **Silence**: Incorporate brief moments of silence during your build, or directly before your drop.
        - **Complementary Sound**: Use a different sound for your primary melody throughout your break and build, then play that melody with a new sound during the drop.
        - **Looping**: Shorten a loop or increase the rate at which a sound is playing to help build tension.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Ensure you fullness of your layering isn’t greater than your drop, as this will reduce it’s impact.

### **RELEASE**

*Use the strategies below to create feelings of impact, resolution, and release of the tension that was built.*

- **Stereo Width**
    - **Strategy**
        - **Full Width**: Have the beginning of your drop, even if for a few moments, be as wide as possible to create the perception of fullness.
        - **Width Variation**: Carefully control the width of your drop sounds to create contrast and interest throughout your drop.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Having your whole drop be fully wide the entire time can lack interest.
        - Too much immediate contrast between a fully narrow build and fully wide drop can be abrasive or overwhelming.
- **Filter**
    - **Strategy**
        - **Open Filter**: Allow the full frequency content from your filtered build sounds to come through during your drop.
        - **Full Low End**: Allow the complete low end of your build sounds to emerge at the beginning of your drop to create fullness.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Your release section should feature the most frequency content, specifically in your low end.
- **Envelope**
    - **Strategy**
        - **Envelope Contrast**: Create contrast in your drop by altering the envelope shape of your primary sound used in your build.
        - **Open Envelope**: Using a more closed envelope sound during your build, then allowing that envelope to open during your drop can be useful in releasing tension.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Maintain some sense of consistency in the sound between your build and drop.
        - Ensure an opened envelope sound in your drop doesn’t make your melody too messy.
- **Volume**
    - **Strategy**
        - **Loud Drop**: Ensure the volume of your drop is louder than the end of your build, to effectively create impact and release tension.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Ensure your drop isn’t so much louder than your build that it comes off as abrasive or overwhelming.
- **Sound Design**
    - **Strategy**
        - **New Sound**: Introduce a new sound in your drop to create contrast from the sounds in your build.
        - **New Layer**: Introduce a new layer if your continuing your primary sound in your drop to help create contrast.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Create some sense of consistency between your build and drop to maintain consistency.
- **FX**
    - **Strategy**
        - **White Noise**: Introduce a brief white noise or exhaust sound to assist in releasing tension in your drop.
        - **Spatial Shots**: Layer in reverb or delay shots underneath primary hits in your drop to help open up space.
    - **Guidelines**
        - Focus on primarily creating release through primary elements, rather than FX. These are just there to support your primary sounds.

Identify a phrasing strategy below that best aligns with your idea and the energy you’re creating. Each strategy refers to a 16-bar section of your drop.
