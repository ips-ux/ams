# Workflow Methodology

- **STEP 0: ORGANIZE**
    - **Directions**
        - **Samples**
            - Collect all your favorite samples into a single location for easy access when you’re producing.
            - We suggest you organize these samples by:
                - Type of Sound (Loops, One Shots)
                - Instrument (Bass, Drums, Synth, FX)
                - Sub-category (Drums -> Kicks, Claps)
            - Start with just 3-5 core sounds for each Instrument.
        - **Plugins**
            - Sort your plugins into specific folders. The structure we recommend is:
                - EQ / Filter / Volume
                - Dynamics (Compression, Limiting)
                - Harmonics (Saturation, Distortion)
                - Instruments (Synths)
                - Space (Reverb, Delay)
                - Stereo Imaging (Chorus, Wideners, ect)
        - **Presets**
            - Save your favorite preset samples within your VST plugins.
            - Ensure all presets are in same octave.
            - Ensure all presets are categorized correctly.
        - **Effect Chains**
            - Sort your favorite Effect Racks and Templates into specific folders accessible within your DAW
            - Experiment with filtering by purpose or utility for easier organization
        - **Templates**
            - Your templates will be used to increase efficiency by providing immediate access to a structure that works best for you.
            - We recommend including:
                - Default drum rack
                - Audio/MIDI channel settings
                - Sidechain channel
                - Master channel inspection
        - **References**
            - Your references will include your favorite tracks which most inspire you from a sound design, mixing, and arrangement perspective.
            - Ideally focus on commercially successful tracks here, as this will ensure the mixing and arrangement translates well to listeners.
            - Start with 5-7 reference tracks.
    - **Recommendations**
        - **Organization is Critical**: Having a unique, selective palette of sounds you can quickly and easily access will enable maximum speed and efficiency in your workflow.
        - **Curating Your Palette**: Chef’s must carefully select and prepare the ingredients in their kitchen. Painters must carefully select and prepare the paints on their palette. Musicians are no different.
        - **Signature Sound**: The sonic palette you create will be the first step in creating your own Signature Sound.

- **STEP 1: SPARK**
    - **Directions**
        - Focus on **core elements only**
        - Work **quickly** and efficiently
        - Less than 30min per idea
        - **Avoid any EQ** or mixing effects
        - **Experiment**, have fun, no expectations
    - **Recommendations**
        - **Building Sandcastles**: Focus on just experimenting, having fun, being creative with no expectations or destination in mind.
        - **Find Uncharted Lands**: Set sail without a destination in mind. Sail for the fun of it. This is the only way to find uncharted lands. Starting with a clear destination in mind is creatively limiting and create expectations difficult to fulfill. This leaves you frustrated and uninspired.
        - **Keep it Simple**: The best tracks only need to do a couple things well. Less is always more. Focus on creating a compelling idea with just a few core sounds.
        - **Speed is Everything:** Focus on more quickly and efficiently at this stage. Your goal is to get your creative idea into your DAW as fast as you can.
        - **Avoid Using EQ:** Create using sounds that occupy different areas of the frequency spectrum. Avoid layering sounds with competing frequencies. This will enable you to have a cleaner, fuller, more complete mix.
        - **5-Minute Rule:** If you’re stuck on a single task more than 5 minutes, simply move on. This will increase your efficiency and ensure you don’t get stuck.
- **STEP 2: EXPAND**
    - **Directions**
        - Select your **best ideas** to expand
        - Expand idea to fill 8-bar section
        - Solidify **phrasing** of your section
        - Focus on **primary elements**
        - Avoid excessive mixing or effects
    - **Recommendations**
        - **Call and Response**: Call is a short phrase that serves as the question. Response is often similar to the call, but may feature variation or elaboration on the original theme. This creates dynamic tension between “performers”, subconsciously creating a conversation the listener is invited to participate in.
        - **Drop Phrasing**: Solidify a phrasing arrangement which creates interest and engagement with the listener. Reference the phrasing arrangement of favorite songs in your style to identify a compelling pattern.
        - **Drop Layering**: Utilize one of the three layering types discussed: Melody Duplication, Melody Variation, Counter Melody. Referencing the layering of your favorite reference tracks can be useful here.
        - **Groove Theory**: Managing the rhythmic feel and timing of your song is essential. Solidify your rhythm, timing, dynamics, and swing in this step. You can apply Groove through Swing, Humanization, Automation, or MIDI effects.
        - **Listening**: Be careful to not over-listen to your ideas at this stage. This will ruin your ability to creatively work on this idea as you subconsciously memorize the existing structure, and slowly close off new creative avenues in your mind.
- **STEP 3: ARRANGE**
    - **Directions**
        - Turn your expanded idea into a full song arrangement
        - Create flexibility for yourself - easier to delete than add later on
        - Utilize Reference Tracks when stuck
    - **Recommendations**
        - **Referencing Arrangements**: Referencing the arrangement of your favorite songs is the most effective way to get inspiration and ideas
        - **Referencing Strategies**: Carefully listen to how elements are introduced, how they are building tension, frequency content of each section, what’s working in this arrangement.
        - **Tension and Release**: A dynamic interplay where musical elements build up anticipation and excitement (tension) and then resolve into a satisfying and impactful section (release), often leading to the drop or chorus. Your arrangement is a critical factor in creating this tension and release.
        - **Tension Strategies**: Most common strategies for building tension include pitch bends, filter sweeps, risers, drum rolls, automations, and additional layer introductions.
- **STEP 4: ELEVATE**
    - **Directions**
        - Every addition should **complement** your existing sounds and melodies
        - Look for ways to add **interest**, **tension**, and **variation**.
        - Identify areas to create **transitions** between sections or sounds
    - **Recommendations**
        - **Layering**: Experiment with adding additional layers, octaves, and timbres to sounds for additional energy and interest.
        - **Reinforce Groove**: Ensure each sound you add or layer serves to reinforce your existing melodic cadence and groove.
        - **Sound Stage Concept**: Only one main instrument can be leading at a certain time. When singer is singing, the guitar, drums, and bass are all there simply to support those vocals. When the guitarist is soloing, all other instruments serve as a foundation to that solo.
        - **Ear Candy**: Explore the techniques below for adding additional interest and complexity to your idea:
            - **Textures**: use atmospheric background layers to add depth to your idea.
            - **Percussive**: incorporate unique, unexpected rhythmic noises to reinforce “the pocket”.
            - **Foley**: use environmental, organic sounds to add unique character to sounds.
            - **Vocals**: utilize small phrases or vocal chops to add a human element.
            - **Resample**: try reusing variations in place of your existing sounds to create interest.
        - **Automations**: These are critical to creating variety and interest in the sounds within your mix:
            - **Volume**: use gradual increases or decreases in volume to add tension or release.
            - **Panning**: move sounds across stereo field for a more interesting listening experience.
            - **Velocity**: experiment with changes in velocity on your key sounds.
            - **Filters**: automate filter cutoff frequencies to gradually add or remove harmonics.
            - **LFOs**: use varying LFO rates to add evolving textures.
            - **Parameters**: experiment with automating any other type of parameter on your plugin or effect
        - **Do Not Over-Produce**: Artists can get stuck for weeks, or even months, trying to “perfect” their idea. These are simply the final touches, the core value of this song has already been created. Move quickly and efficiently here to the next stage, and do not “over﻿-produce” your idea.
